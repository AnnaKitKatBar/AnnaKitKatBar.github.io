Audio Visualizer Software
In my last quarter of college, I was finally able to take a computer graphics class. Needless to say, I fell in love with creating things that you could visually see on screen. I've always held an interest in CGI and special effects in movies and TV shows, so I always saw this class as a first step into learning more about how to do things like that. For my final project, I decided to make an audio visualizer because I've always loved music and thought it would be cool to incorporate that. I also like the real-time responsiveness that an audio visualizer does and the idea that it enables people to see sound.
To help with the audio analysis, I used the FMOD library. With this frequency analysis, I was able to visualize the different frequencies and their intensities as well as visualize when a heavy bass or crashing snare beat sounded. To see more in depth about what I did, here is a link to the project web page I made to turn in for my final project.

It runs smoother on my computer and the beat detection is more synched, but out of all the other screen recorders I've tried, this was the best back then. Hardware
I loved the idea of being able to see sound so much, that for another class, I also made an audio visualizer of sorts. This time, completely in hardware.
For my industrial manufacturing class, I made a transistor based audio spectrum analyzer. I used a preexisting circuit schematic and designed the components and PCB circuit layout using DipTrace. I then got the final PCB fabricated, populated the board, assembled and soldered the components by hand, and debugged the finished product.
In a nutshell, what this does is take in an audio input signal and various LEDs turn on and off depending on the frequencies detected at that time. After the signal goes in, the first part of the circuit amplifies the signal, there are then three frequency filters that the signal goes through to represent the three frequency bands of low, mid, and high. If a signal is able to pass through one of these filters, the AC signal gets rectified to a DC signal, which then turns on the LED driver which lights up the corresponding LEDs. Here is a link to a short report for more details, schematics, and figures for how this hardware audio viusalizer works.
